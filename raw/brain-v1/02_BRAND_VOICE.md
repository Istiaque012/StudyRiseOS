## 2. Tagline System

### 2.1 Primary Tagline

> **Plan Today. Rise Tomorrow.**

This is the brand tagline. It appears on the logo lockup (as a layout element, not baked into the mark), landing page hero, email headers, about pages, and anywhere the brand is introduced for the first time.

**Why it works:** Two-beat rhythm. Present-tense verb → future-tense promise. Directly echoes the brand name ("Rise"). Implies structure (planning) and aspiration (rising) without hype.

**Usage rules:**
- Always styled: Inter 300, uppercase, letter-spacing `0.15em`, colour Slate `#64748B`
- Never modify the wording. Never add punctuation beyond the two periods.
- Never pair with a second tagline in the same visual frame.

---

### 2.2 Supporting Taglines (contextual)

These are not replacements for the primary tagline. They are **short punches** for specific surfaces where "Plan Today. Rise Tomorrow." is either too long or doesn't fit the context. Pick the one that matches the context. Never use more than one at a time.

#### Brand-Level (general positioning)

| Line | Best for | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Study smarter. Not longer.** | Social media bios, app store subtitle, ad headlines | Benefit-first. Contrasts with "grind culture." Immediately signals what category StudyRise lives in. |
| **Your exam. Your plan. Your pace.** | Landing pages, onboarding screens, email welcome series | Emphasises personalisation and student agency. Three-beat rhythm matches the primary tagline. |
| **Structure turns effort into results.** | Blog CTAs, content marketing, about page copy | Positions StudyRise's core thesis: planning > raw effort. Appeals to students who already study hard but feel lost. |
| **The study planner that thinks ahead.** | App store description (first line), PPC ad copy, social ads | Functional descriptor with personality. "Thinks ahead" hints at AI + spaced repetition without jargon. |

---

### 2.3 Tagline Decision Tree

Deciding which line to use:

```
Is this the brand's first introduction to this person?
  → YES → Use the primary tagline: "Plan Today. Rise Tomorrow."
  → NO → Is this a specific audience segment?
           → YES → Use the matching audience-specific line
           → NO → Pick the brand-level supporting line that matches the surface
```

### 2.4 Lines We Will Never Use

These fail the brand voice test. Listed here so they stay dead.

- ~~"Ace every exam"~~ — overpromises; violates Trustworthy pillar
- ~~"The ultimate study app"~~ — generic hype; on the Avoid list

- ~~"Learn anything, anywhere"~~ — not what we do; we plan and track, not teach content
- ~~"The future of learning"~~ — grandiose for an early-access product; violates honesty test

## 3. Voice Character

### 3.1 StudyRise Sounds Like…

Imagine a senior resident at a teaching hospital who also happens to be good with a spreadsheet. They've been through the exam you're about to sit. They don't sugarcoat the difficulty, but they never make you feel stupid for asking. They give you a concrete next step, not a vague pep talk.

**The voice is:**
- A calm, sharp coach — not a cheerleader
- A knowledgeable peer — not an authority figure lecturing down
- An honest advisor — not a salesperson
- Quietly confident — not performatively humble or boastful

### 3.2 The Comparison Test

Use this to gut-check any piece of copy before publishing.

| StudyRise sounds like… | StudyRise does NOT sound like… |
|---|---|
| A senior resident explaining a study plan over coffee | A startup founder pitching investors |
| A well-organised friend who shares their spreadsheet | A motivational speaker on stage |
| A tutoring centre's best tutor — direct, patient, practical | A generic chatbot ("Great question! Let me help you with that!") |
| The clear, structured voice of a well-written textbook introduction | The breathless energy of a product launch email |
| Ali Abdaal explaining a technique — evidence, examples, no fluff | A TikTok influencer screaming "THIS CHANGED MY LIFE" |

### 3.3 The Wince Test

Before publishing any claim, ask: *Would a knowledgeable doctor or teacher reading this nod, or wince?*

If an expert would wince — a too-neat statistic, an overstated benefit, an oversimplified medical fact — fix it. StudyRise's entire competitive advantage in a medical niche is being the source experts don't wince at. **Protect that above all.**

## 4. The Five Voice Pillars

The pillars are constant across every audience and every surface. They are non-negotiable.

### Pillar 1: Structured

**What it means:** Clear hierarchy. One idea per paragraph. The reader always knows where they are and what comes next. Headings answer questions. Sections build on each other. Nothing competes for attention.

**In practice:**
- Lead every section with its answer. The first sentence under any heading should answer the heading's implied question.
- Use numbered steps for processes, not vague narrative.
- When explaining a concept, give the rule first, then the example, then the exception.

**Example (good):** "Spaced repetition works by increasing the gap between reviews as your recall improves. Review a card at 1 day, then 3 days, then 7, then 21. If you forget, the gap resets."

**Example (bad):** "Have you ever noticed how you forget things you studied just days ago? It's really frustrating, isn't it? Well, there's a fascinating solution called spaced repetition that researchers have been studying for decades…"

---

### Pillar 2: Momentum-Driven

**What it means:** Every piece of content moves the reader toward doing something — a plan, a technique, a decision. Never just "interesting."

**In practice:**
- End every major section with a next step, not a summary.
- Frame features as actions: "Track your progress" not "Progress tracking feature."
- Progress language is visual and physical: *ahead of schedule, falling behind, recover, catch up, on track*.

**Example (good):** "You're 2 days behind. Here's how to recover."

**Example (bad):** "Progress is a journey, and every step counts."

---

### Pillar 3: Trustworthy

**What it means:** Honest about difficulty, pass rates, and trade-offs. Never overpromises. "Overdue means overdue." Data is shown accurately — no inflated scores.

**In practice:**
- If research can't confirm a fact, leave it out. A single fabricated pass rate costs more trust than a dozen missing details.
- Cite real sources. "Research shows…" is weak. "Ebbinghaus (1885) demonstrated…" or "A 2024 meta-analysis in Medical Education found…" is strong.
- Acknowledge when something is hard. "The AMC MCQ has a roughly 50% pass rate for first-time candidates" is more useful and more trustworthy than "You can definitely pass!"

**Example (good):** "This schedule is tight. If you're working full-time, you'll need to adjust the daily target or extend the timeline by 2–3 weeks."

**Example (bad):** "Follow this plan and you're guaranteed to pass!"

---

### Pillar 4: Quietly Ambitious

**What it means:** Takes the reader's goal seriously. Encouraging without being hollow. Motivational without clichés. The tone says: *I believe you can do this, and here's the structure to prove it.*

**In practice:**
- Encouragement is tied to specific, earned progress — not generic cheerleading.
- Never trivialise the goal. Passing a licensing exam is genuinely hard. Honour that.
- The brand's ambition shows through the *quality of the plan*, not through exclamation marks.

**Example (good):** "Phase 1 complete."

**Example (bad):** "Amazing work, superstar! 🔥"

---

### Pillar 5: Intelligent

**What it means:** Evidence-led. Cites real research and real sources. Explains *why*, not just *what*. The reader leaves smarter, not just informed.

**In practice:**
- Explain the mechanism, not just the advice. "Use spaced repetition" → "Spaced repetition exploits the spacing effect — your brain strengthens a memory each time it's forced to retrieve it after a gap."
- Reference established research naturally, not as decoration.
- Use precise language. "About 4 months" is better than "a few months." "20 questions per day" is better than "a good number of questions."

**Example (good):** "Active recall — testing yourself rather than re-reading — consistently outperforms passive review in controlled studies. A 2013 meta-analysis by Dunlosky et al. rated it as the highest-utility learning technique."

**Example (bad):** "Studies show that active recall is better. It's science!"

## 5. Audience Voices

The five pillars stay constant. The **register** — level of formality, assumed knowledge, emotional tone, and cultural context — shifts per audience. Each audience below has its own mini voice guide.

---

### 5.1 [EXAM] — IMG Candidates (AMC MCQ · PLAB 1 · USMLE)

**Who they are:** Qualified doctors — often practising or recently graduated — studying for a licensing exam in a foreign country. They are professionals, not students in the traditional sense. Many are studying while working, managing visa timelines, and supporting families. The stakes are existential: career, immigration status, financial stability.

**Register:** Peer-to-peer professional. Think colleague, not teacher.

**Assumed knowledge:** High. They know medicine. They don't need basic science explained. What they need is exam-specific strategy, scheduling, and resource guidance.

**Emotional tone:** Respectful, direct, stress-aware. Acknowledge the difficulty without dwelling on it. Don't be grim, but don't be breezy either. These are adults making serious sacrifices.

#### Voice Do's — [EXAM]

- Address them as peers. "As a doctor, you already know the pharmacology — the AMC tests *application*, not recall."
- Be specific about exam mechanics. "The AMC MCQ is 150 questions in 3.5 hours. That's 84 seconds per question."
- Acknowledge the real constraints. "If you're working full-time, a 6-month timeline is more realistic than 4."
- Use professional-register vocabulary. *Candidate, clinician, exam window, pass standard, clinical vignette.*

#### Voice Don'ts — [EXAM]

- ❌ Don't condescend. "Let me explain what pharmacology is…" — they have a medical degree.
- ❌ Don't use student slang. "Crushing it!" / "Study vibes" / "Grind mode" — they're professionals.
- ❌ Don't trivialise the stakes. "Don't stress, you've got this!" — they're making a career-defining bet.
- ❌ Don't overpromise pass outcomes. "Follow this plan and you'll definitely pass" — pass rates exist for a reason.

#### Example Copy — [EXAM]

**Blog intro (AMC MCQ article):**
> The AMC MCQ pass standard changed in 2026. If you're planning your attempt, that affects how many questions you need to get right and which resources to prioritise. This guide breaks the exam into a 16-week schedule built around spaced repetition — realistic for candidates working part-time, tight but achievable full-time.

**Social post:**
> 150 questions. 3.5 hours. 84 seconds each.
> The AMC MCQ doesn't test what you know — it tests what you can apply under pressure.
> Here's a 16-week plan that accounts for that. [link]

**CTA:**
> Building a study schedule around shift work is hard to get right by hand. StudyRise generates one from your exam date — try it free.

### 5.2 [MBBS-BD] — Bangladeshi Medical Students

**Who they are:** Medical students at Bangladeshi institutions following the BMDC 2021 curriculum. They navigate a phased professional examination system (1st Prof through Final Prof), supplementary exams, continuous assessment cards, and send-up exams. Many face documented high levels of academic stress and mental health challenges.

**Register:** Warm, grounded, locally aware. Think older sibling who went through the same system, not foreign consultant.

**Assumed knowledge:** Medium to high on medicine, variable on study methodology. They know their curriculum but may not have been exposed to spaced repetition or structured planning tools.

**Emotional tone:** Calm and reassuring. **Deliberately restrained.** No pressure framing, no urgency tactics, no gamification language (streaks, leaderboards, points). These students face enough pressure from the system itself — StudyRise should feel like relief, not another source of stress.

#### Voice Do's — [MBBS-BD]

- Reflect what they actually do. "Send-up exams," "supplementary," "BMDC phases" — use their terminology.
- Write in clear, simple sentence structures. Avoid English idioms that don't translate well to Bangladeshi English.
- Be calm. The tone should feel like a deep breath, not a countdown clock.
- Give practical, local context. "Your 2nd Professional covers Pathology, Microbiology, Pharmacology, and Forensic Medicine" (only when verified against BMDC source).
- Use BDT when referencing pricing. Localise examples.

#### Voice Don'ts — [MBBS-BD]

- ❌ **Never use gamification language.** No streaks, no points, no leaderboards, no "Don't break the chain!" — these add pressure to a stressed population.
- ❌ **Never guess a BMDC curriculum fact.** If you can't verify which subject sits in which professional phase, leave it out and flag it with `[VERIFY: ...]`. A wrong phase mapping destroys credibility with this exact audience.
- ❌ Don't use imported study clichés. "The 80/20 rule!" / "Study hacks!" — these sound foreign and disconnected from their reality.
- ❌ Don't assume they have unlimited internet bandwidth or access to expensive resources.
- ❌ Don't be chipper. "You've got this! 😊" reads as tone-deaf when someone is facing a supplementary exam.

#### Example Copy — [MBBS-BD]

**Blog intro (BMDC assessment guide):**
> The continuous assessment card is a requirement for every MBBS student in Bangladesh. It tracks your attendance, internal assessments, and clinical performance across each professional phase. If the card isn't complete, you can't sit your professional exam — regardless of how well you've studied the material. This guide explains what goes into the card, what supervisors are looking for, and how to stay on top of it without last-minute panic.

**Social post:**
> Your continuous assessment card isn't extra paperwork — it's your exam ticket.
> Here's what needs to be in it, phase by phase.
> [link]

**CTA:**
> StudyRise maps the BMDC 2021 curriculum into a day-by-day plan for each professional phase. Start free — no card required.

### 5.3 [UNI] — University Students

**Who they are:** Undergraduate and postgraduate students studying any discipline, doing semester-based coursework and exams. They balance multiple subjects, assignments, social life, and often part-time work. They're digitally native and compare apps instinctively.

**Register:** Friendly, energetic, practical. The most casual register. Think smart friend who figured out a good system, not an institution.

**Assumed knowledge:** Variable on subject matter, generally low on study methodology. Many have never used spaced repetition or structured planning.

**Emotional tone:** Light but grounded. Slightly more casual. Focus on quick, tangible wins — "you'll feel the difference this week." Humour is allowed in small doses (dry, never sarcastic). Energy is allowed (but no hype).

#### Voice Do's — [UNI]

- Focus on immediate, tangible results. "20 cards every morning before your first class."
- Use relatable student scenarios. "It's week 10 and you have three exams in five days."
- Be slightly more casual. Contractions, shorter sentences, conversational rhythm.
- Show, don't tell. If a technique works, show the schedule or the before/after.

#### Voice Don'ts — [UNI]

- ❌ Don't be patronising. They're adults, not children. No "Now, I know studying can be hard…"
- ❌ Don't use hollow motivation. "Believe in yourself!" — they need a plan, not a poster.
- ❌ Don't try to sound "Gen Z." Forced slang ages instantly. Write clearly and the audience will come to you.
- ❌ Don't over-reference specific majors unless the article is about that discipline.

#### Example Copy — [UNI]

**Blog intro (study schedule article):**
> A study schedule works when it accounts for everything you actually do — not just study. Classes, assignments, part-time work, the commute, the gym. If your plan ignores those, it'll collapse by Wednesday. Here's how to build one that holds up.

**Social post:**
> The problem isn't that you don't study enough.
> It's that you don't know what to study *when*.
> A schedule fixes that. Here's how to build one in 20 minutes. [link]

**CTA:**
> StudyRise builds your study schedule around your real life — classes, deadlines, and all. Try it free.

### 5.4 [ALL] — Top of Funnel (cross-audience)

**Who they are:** Anyone searching for general study techniques — spaced repetition, active recall, Pomodoro, time management. They may not know what StudyRise is. They may not even know they need a study planner. This is often someone's first contact with the brand.

**Register:** Clear and welcoming. Assume zero prior knowledge. Define terms the first time they appear. Universal — no audience-specific jargon.

**Emotional tone:** Approachable, informative, gently compelling. The goal is to earn trust and demonstrate expertise, not to sell. The product mention is natural and late in the piece.

#### Voice Do's — [ALL]

- Define every technical term on first use. "Spaced repetition — reviewing material at increasing intervals to strengthen memory."
- Use universally relatable examples. "That textbook chapter you read twice but can't recall a week later."
- Write for someone who has never planned their study before.
- Let the value of the content sell the product. If the article is good enough, the CTA earns itself.

#### Voice Don'ts — [ALL]

- ❌ Don't assume the reader is a medical student. "When you're studying for Step 1…" — this is top of funnel; they might be studying for a biology final.
- ❌ Don't use exam-specific terminology without defining it.
- ❌ Don't hard-sell. The CTA pattern is: *establish value → show how StudyRise does this specific thing → soft invitation.*
- ❌ Don't write filler introductions. "In today's fast-paced world…" / "We all know that…" — start with the answer.

#### Example Copy — [ALL]

**Blog intro (spaced repetition article):**
> Spaced repetition is a study technique where you review material at increasing intervals — 1 day, then 3 days, then 7, then 21. Each time your brain is forced to retrieve the information after a gap, the memory gets stronger. It's one of the most studied techniques in cognitive psychology, and it works for everything from medical exams to learning a language.

**Social post:**
> You reviewed 50 pages yesterday.
> How many can you recall right now?
>
> That gap between effort and retention is the whole problem.
> Spaced repetition closes it. Here's how. [link]

**CTA:**
> Building a spaced-repetition schedule by hand is tedious and easy to get wrong. StudyRise generates one automatically from your exam date — try it free.

## 6. Copy Rules by Surface

### 6.1 Blog Articles & Landing Pages

- **Lead with the answer.** First sentence answers the heading's implied question.
- **Short paragraphs.** 2–4 sentences max. Walls of text lose mobile readers.
- **Vary sentence length.** Mix short punches with longer explanatory sentences. All-medium reads robotic.
- **One concrete example per concept.** "Study consistently" → "20 cards every morning before your first class."
- **CTA placement:** One natural mention mid-article where genuinely relevant. One clear invitation near the end. Never more than two in an informational piece.
- **CTA pattern:** *Establish value → show how StudyRise does this specific thing → soft invitation.*
- **Internal links:** Descriptive anchors only. "PLAB 1 study planner" — never "read more" or "click here."
- **External links:** At least one authoritative source per article. Link to the evidence.
- **Meta descriptions:** 140–160 characters. Benefit + differentiator + light hook. Not keyword-stuffed.

### 6.2 UI Copy (in-app)

- **Direct and plain.** "3 tasks due today" — not "You've got this, champ! 🎉"
- **Honest about progress.** Show the real number, even when it's low.
- **No exclamation marks in UI copy.** Reserve energy for actual milestones (exam complete, plan finished).
- **Status messages follow this pattern:**
  - Ahead: "On track · X days ahead of schedule"
  - On time: "On track · right on schedule"
  - Behind: "Behind by X days — consider rescheduling"
- **Never assume the exam.** Language applies to any study goal, not just medical licensing.
- **Error messages are helpful, not cute.** "Couldn't save your plan. Check your connection and try again." — not "Oops! Something went sideways! 🙈"

### 6.3 Social Media

- **Platform tone adjustments:**
  - **X/Twitter:** Tightest. One idea per post. Punch line or insight, then link. No threads longer than 4 posts.
  - **LinkedIn:** Professional register. Think [EXAM] voice by default. Data and evidence land well here. Longer-form is fine.
  - **Instagram:** Visual-first. Caption supports the visual — don't write an essay. [UNI] register works best here.
  - **Reddit/Quora:** Community-first. Be genuinely helpful. The product mention is an aside, not the point. Use relevant audience voice for the subreddit.
  - **Facebook Groups (BD communities):** [MBBS-BD] voice. Warm, calm, locally relevant. Bangla mixed in sparingly where natural.

- **Social copy rules:**
  - One idea per post. Not three.
  - Open with a pattern interrupt — a number, a question, a counterintuitive statement.
  - No hashtag walls. Maximum 3–5 relevant hashtags. None in the body copy — add below or in a comment.
  - Never use emojis as structural elements (🔥 to mean emphasis, 👆 to mean "look above"). One accent emoji maximum, and only if the tone calls for it.
  - Every post earns its CTA. If the content doesn't naturally lead to StudyRise, don't force one. Helpful posts without a CTA build more trust than constant shilling.

- **Social bio templates:**
  - **Primary/general:** `StudyRise — Study smarter. Not longer. | Smart study planning & tracking for serious students. Try it free — www.studyrise.app`
  - **[EXAM]-focused account:** `StudyRise — Built for doctors studying across borders. | AMC · PLAB · USMLE study planning. Try it free — www.studyrise.app`
  - **[MBBS-BD]-focused:** `StudyRise — Study planning for every MBBS phase. | BMDC 2021 curriculum. Day-by-day scheduling. Try it free — www.studyrise.app`
  - **[UNI]-focused:** `StudyRise — Finals don't plan themselves. | Study planner + tracker for university students. Try it free — www.studyrise.app`

### 6.4 Email

- **Subject lines:** 6–10 words. Benefit or curiosity, never clickbait. No ALL CAPS. No 🔥 emoji.
  - Good: "Your 16-week AMC MCQ schedule (free template)"
  - Good: "The spacing effect: why you forget and how to fix it"
  - Bad: "🚀 YOU WON'T BELIEVE this study hack!!!"
  - Bad: "StudyRise Newsletter #14"

- **Welcome email tone:** Warm, concise, immediately useful. Introduce StudyRise in one sentence. Offer one concrete next action. Don't overwhelm with features.

- **Newsletter tone:** Same as [ALL] blog voice. Lead with the most useful thing. Product updates are secondary to useful content.

- **Pricing framing in email:** "You have full access to every feature — free for 30 days, no card required." Never create urgency around the 30-day window. See `11_PRICING_MODEL.md` for approved phrases.

### 6.5 Paid Ads

- **Headline formula:** Problem → mechanism → outcome. Or: Specific + honest + actionable.
  - Good: "150 AMC questions/day × 16 weeks = exam ready. Free study planner."
  - Good: "Your exam has a date. Now give it a plan."
  - Bad: "The ULTIMATE Study App — Download NOW!"
  - Bad: "Revolutionary AI-Powered Study Platform"

- **Description copy:** One sentence of specificity. One sentence of proof or differentiation. CTA.
  - "StudyRise plans your study schedule around spaced repetition and your real exam date. Used by medical graduates preparing for AMC, PLAB, and USMLE. Try it free."

- **Ad rules:**
  - Never claim guaranteed pass outcomes.
  - Never use fear-based framing ("Don't fail your exam!").
  - Never use "free trial" — it's 30 days of the real product, not a trial. Say "try it free" or "free for 30 days."
  - Display URLs always point to `www.studyrise.app`.

### 6.6 App Store

- **App name:** StudyRise — Smart Study Planner

- **Subtitle (30 chars):** "Study smarter. Not longer."

- **First line of description (the visible part):** "Plan your study schedule around your exam date, your subjects, and your real life. StudyRise uses spaced repetition and smart scheduling to help you study the right things at the right time — whether you're preparing for a medical licensing exam, university finals, or any structured study goal."

- **App store rules:**
  - Lead with what the app *does*, not what it *is*.
  - Use "you/your" — write to one person.
  - Don't keyword-stuff the description. One natural mention of key terms is enough.
  - Screenshot captions follow UI voice: short, factual, benefit-oriented.
  - Never use "best" or "ultimate" or "#1" — these require substantiation and read as untrustworthy.

## 7. Word Bank & Anti-Patterns

### 7.1 Words We Use

| Word | Why |
|---|---|
| plan, build, track, revise, pass, master | Plain, direct verbs. Action-oriented. |
| realistic, evidence-based, step-by-step, sustainable | Honest qualifiers. Signal trustworthiness. |
| schedule, timeline, pace, progress, readiness | Core product vocabulary. Momentum language. |
| recover, catch up, adjust, reschedule | Behind-schedule language. Honest, not panicky. |
| you, your | Second person. Write to one person. |
| spaced repetition, active recall, Pomodoro | Named techniques. Use precisely. Define on first use in [ALL] content. |

### 7.2 Words We Avoid

| Word/Phrase | Why |
|---|---|
| revolutionary, game-changing, ultimate | Hype. Overused and unsubstantiated. |
| hack, secret, trick | Clickbait register. Undermines Intelligent pillar. |
| crush it, grind, beast mode, no excuses | Hollow motivation. Antithetical to the calm-coach voice. |
| "In today's fast-paced world…" | Filler intro. Start with the answer. |
| "We all know that…" | Presumptuous filler. |
| "It's worth noting that…" | AI tell. Cut it. |
| "In conclusion…" | AI tell. The structure should make this unnecessary. |
| "Let's dive in…" | AI tell + cliché. |
| "Furthermore…" used as padding | Connective filler. Restructure instead. |
| "Don't forget to…" | Nagging tone. Incompatible with calm-coach voice. |
| premium, unlock | Pushy framing. Incompatible with calm-coach voice. Use "Pro" for the tier name. |
| upgrade | Permitted ONLY on the pricing page and in-app billing. Banned in blog, social, email, and ad copy. See `11_PRICING_MODEL.md`. |
| free trial | It's 30 days of the real product, not a trial. Say "free for 30 days" or "try it free." |
| streak, leaderboard, points, badges, XP | Gamification. Banned for [MBBS-BD]; use with extreme caution elsewhere. |
| buddy, bestie, fam | Overfamiliar. We're a coach, not a friend. |
| guaranteed, definitely will pass | Overpromise. Never guarantee outcomes. |

### 7.3 Brand-Name Rules

- **Always:** StudyRise (one word, capital S, capital R)
- **Never:** Study Rise, Studyrise, studyrise, STUDYRISE, SR (except in internal code variables)
- **In sentences:** "StudyRise helps you…" — not "the StudyRise app helps you…" (unless specifically discussing the mobile app vs. the web platform)
- **Possessive:** "StudyRise's spaced repetition engine" — the apostrophe-s is fine

### 7.4 Pricing Copy Rules

- ✅ "Basic features free forever. Pro features free for 30 days."

- ✅ "Upgrade to Pro" — pricing page and in-app billing only
- ❌ Never say "premium," "unlock," or "free trial"
